How does the gender-data gap impact urban design?

Urban planning is the process of designing and developing urban areas to meet the needs of a community, using the framework of land use, infrastructure, and human services to create fulfilling, livable, sustainable, efficient, and resilient cities. Urban planning and urban design would be nothing without the societal systems in place that enable the weaknesses of vulnerable cities. Architecture plays a large role in urban planning and design, and often, the blind client-designer dynamic contributes to the inefficiencies of building and urban design that fails to consider the needs of women and instead, build around the needs of men. For example, in Stockholm, a city that tends to receive copious amounts of snow, primarily targets snow-removal for roads instead of paths largely taken by women and children like entries to day cares, footpaths, and cycle paths. Thankfully, Stockholm took note of this issue and began to prioritize snow-plowing pavements. In Bangladesh, poor cyclone shelter design led to a death toll among women that was 5x higher than men due to its lack of sanitation facilities, ramps for pregnant women, and private spaces for lactating mothers. With cramped spaces, sexual harassment occurred, and women were discouraged from using such facilities which led to more death from cyclone and floors in 1991 (Sharmin & Islam, 2013).
